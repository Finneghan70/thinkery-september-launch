The conceptual framework
About this guide
The Teach Daily framework
This guide is built on Teach Daily's Teaching Structure, developed by Gail Boushey and Allison Behne, evolved from the original Daily 5, Reading Strategies (CAFE), and Math frameworks. The Teaching Structure has four components:

1. Whole Group Lesson (10–15 min) — one clear idea, taught briefly
2. Set a Purpose (under 2 min) — students name how they will apply the learning
3. Independent Practice + Responsive Teaching (15–30 min, simultaneous) — students work; teacher confers
4. Share & Reflect (5–10 min) — students do the thinking in the review

September's launch builds the conditions for steps 3 and 4 to be genuine. Without established independence and engagement, the work period is compliance, not learning.
The six layers of the September launch
These layers are not weeks — they overlap and build on each other. The sequence tells you what to introduce first. You are always tending all active layers simultaneously.
Why this order?
Relationships before routines. A student who doesn't feel safe will not take risks in their learning. The first two days are deliberately relational — not procedural. Every routine you teach after Day 2 will land better because students feel known.

Routines before independence. The transitions lesson on Day 3 is not about transitions. It is a low-stakes rehearsal of the 10 Steps framework, using a simple routine so that when you use the same structure for Read to Self on Day 9, students already know how it works.

Foundation lessons before the 10 Steps. You cannot build an I-chart for independence until students know what independence means. You cannot build engagement intervals until students have language for the continuum. The five foundation lessons are not prerequisites in a bureaucratic sense — they are the vocabulary that makes the 10 Steps coherent.

Independent before collaborative. Students who cannot sustain independent engagement will not sustain collaborative engagement. Collaboration is harder, not easier. It adds responsibility to others. Launch independent structures first, establish them firmly, then add collaboration on top.

This framework teaches any routine. Transitions, Read to Self, Math by Myself, collaborative learning, book shopping — any behaviour you want to become automatic gets the same 10-step treatment. The content of the I-chart changes. The structure never does.

Conferring in September
This tab is a how-to guide, not a recording tool. Everything here explains the three conferring moves, their sequence, and how to structure each one. What you actually observe and record during conferences — student strengths, goals, next steps, touch points — lives in the Thinkery Assessment Tool. September conferring is about behaviour, not content. You do not begin Reading Strategies (CAFE) goal-setting conferences until students can sustain independent practice. The first two weeks, your job during the work period is to stay out of the way. Week 3, you begin Behavior Conferences and Quick Checks. Goal-setting conferences begin in Week 4 once engagement is established.
📋 Three conferring moves — in sequence Behaviour Conference → Quick Check → Goal-Setting ▼
🔢 The 7 Steps from Assessment to Instruction Use when beginning goal-setting conferences in Week 4 ▼
Use this when you are ready to begin goal-setting conferences (Week 4). Not before. Rushing to content conferring before independence is established is the most common mistake in launching Daily 5.
🎯 The Lesson Framework during conferring Understand → Prepare → Teach → Support — every conference, 5–8 minutes ▼
Every conference follows the same 4-stage structure as the Foundation Lessons. In a conference, these four stages take 5–8 minutes total.
Stage 1
Observe & Plan (Understand)
Sit beside the student. Watch for 20–30 seconds before speaking. What are they doing? What is working? What does their body language tell you? Identify one teaching point.
Stage 2
Engage & Connect (Prepare)
Set a positive tone. Connect to the student’s reading, writing, or thinking. Name what you noticed that’s going well. State your purpose for this conference in one sentence.
Stage 3
Teach One Thing (Teach)
Use one instructional move: Explicitly Explain, Model, Think Aloud, Guide/Prompt, Offer Advice, or Feedback. Not two. One clear teaching point.
Stage 4
Practice & Record (Support)
Student names what they will work on in their own words. Record in your conferring notebook: strength, teaching point, next step, touch point date. 30 seconds to record.
Instructional Pivot
Did my instruction work? Yes / No. If no: adjust the setting, materials, instructional practice, or cognitive process. Do not repeat the same approach and expect a different result.
The conceptual foundation
Understanding Engagement
📊 The Continuum of Engagement Disengaged → Passive → Engaged ▼
Disengaged
Off-task. Avoiding the work. May appear to be working but is not applying thinking.
→
Passive
Present and compliant but not actively engaged. Going through the motions.
→
Engaged
Actively applying thinking. Reading, writing, problem-solving with genuine focus.
Passive is not the goal. A quiet classroom where everyone appears to be working is not evidence of engagement. The Continuum gives both teachers and students language to name where they are — honestly — and what to reach for.
💬 Student self-assessment questions Five questions students ask themselves during the work period ▼
Teach these five questions explicitly during Foundation Lesson 2. Students learn to ask themselves these questions during the work period — not just when prompted by the teacher.
🔁 Engagement by mode Receptive and expressive — for Grade 4/5 students ▼
For Grade 4/5 students, engagement can be described in two modes. Use this language in conferences and reflections once the vocabulary is established.
Receptive Mode
Taking in information
Reading, listening, viewing. Engagement in receptive mode means actively processing — asking questions, connecting, visualizing, monitoring understanding. Not just eyes on the page.
Expressive Mode
Producing and sharing
Speaking, writing, visually representing. Engagement in expressive mode means actively constructing meaning — drafting, revising, explaining thinking. Not just filling a page.
🌡️ The barometer student The first student to break engagement — and what to do ▼
Every class has a barometer student — the first student to break engagement. This student measures the “weather” in the room. They are not the problem. They are the data. When the barometer student breaks engagement, it means the work period has reached its limit for the whole class — even the students who appear to still be working.

What to do: Use the quiet signal. Come back to the group. Do a brief group check-in (Step 10 of the 10 Steps). Do not single out the barometer student — thank the whole group and record the time. This is your baseline for tomorrow.
Building engagement over time: Start with 3–5 minutes in Week 1. Add 2–3 minutes per successful session. Use a visual timer students can see. Celebrate milestones as a group. By the end of September, the goal for Grade 4/5 is 20–25 minutes of sustained engaged independent reading.
